## 2. Document Purpose

### 2.1 Purpose Statement

This document defines the recycling lifecycle and admission boundaries of the Assembler System Independent Pipeline.

It answers the question:

```text
When may material enter an Independent Pipeline, when must the pipeline retain
stable outbound payload material, when may that material be released, and when
may internal containers, slots, and lifecycle structures be recycled without
claiming downstream delivery, ABI passage, add-on projection, or final receiver
consumption?
````

The document protects the pipeline from two opposite failures:

1. accepting material when it cannot safely own or process it,
2. recycling internal structures before ASIP-owned exposure and release obligations are satisfied.

It also prevents a third failure:

```text
treating ASIP recycling as proof that the payload was delivered downstream.
```

### 2.2 Relationship to ASIP-001

`ASIP-001` established that the Independent Pipeline is the unit of correctness, ownership, operation, and encapsulation.

This document specializes that principle into lifecycle terms:

```text
The pipeline owns admission, retention, release, and recycling only within
ASIP authority.
```

The pipeline does not own the lifecycle of destinations.

### 2.3 Relationship to ASIP-002

`ASIP-002` defined the internal mechanics of admission, preparation, containers, slots, waiting lists, round managers, internal dispatch readiness, and stabilization.

This document now defines when those internal structures may be reused.

It also defines the boundary between:

```text
internal lifecycle safety
and
external delivery lifecycle
```

### 2.4 Relationship to ASIP-003

`ASIP-003` defined stable outbound payload exposure as ASIP’s terminal boundary.

This document defines what happens around that boundary:

```text
stable payload produced
→ exposed
→ retained
→ bridge-facing claim or expiration
→ ASIP release
→ internal recycling
```

The lifecycle does not extend to final receivers.

### 2.5 Relationship to ASIP-004

`ASIP-004` established that configuration may tune ASIP behavior but must not encode destination semantics.

This document applies that rule to lifecycle configuration.

Configuration may define:

1. admission capacity,
2. retention budget,
3. expiration policy,
4. release rules,
5. recycling thresholds.

Configuration must not define:

1. database retry,
2. telemetry export retry,
3. receiver acknowledgement,
4. ABI completion semantics,
5. downstream persistence lifecycle.

### 2.6 Relationship to ASIP-005

`ASIP-005` established the execution baseline:

```text
one writer
→ one independent pipeline instance
→ deterministic internal execution
→ stable outbound payload exposure
```

It previewed release and recycling as ASIP lifecycle transitions, not proof of downstream delivery.

This document fully defines that lifecycle.

## 4. Core Thesis

### 4.1 Primary Thesis

```text
Admission decides whether material may enter ASIP authority.

Retention preserves stable outbound payload material until ASIP-owned release
conditions are satisfied.

Release ends ASIP’s obligation to retain that material.

Recycling reuses internal structures after release.

None of these lifecycle states proves downstream delivery, ABI passage,
projection, persistence, export, or receiver consumption.
```

### 4.2 Expanded Thesis

The Independent Pipeline must be conservative about lifecycle reuse.

The pipeline may recycle internal structures only after the material occupying those structures is no longer required by ASIP-owned obligations.

Those obligations may include:

1. completing preparation,
2. completing stabilization,
3. exposing a stable outbound payload,
4. retaining exposed material until bridge-facing claim,
5. retaining exposed material until configured expiration,
6. preserving diagnostic trace where required,
7. preventing mutation of exposed payload meaning.

The pipeline must not wait for final destination behavior unless a later ASCC/ASOP/ASAO contract explicitly reports a boundary-safe release signal that ASIP is allowed to consume.

### 4.3 Lifecycle Rule

```text
ASIP lifecycle safety is about internal ownership, exposure, release, and reuse.
It is not downstream delivery lifecycle.
```

## 5. Admission Boundary Model

### 5.1 Admission Boundary Definition

The admission boundary is the point where material becomes owned by an Independent Pipeline instance for internal processing.

Before admission, material is outside ASIP ownership.

After admission, the pipeline is responsible for processing or rejecting it according to ASIP-owned rules.

### 5.2 Admission Means

Admission means:

1. the pipeline has accepted material into its internal lifecycle,
2. the material may participate in validation,
3. the material may receive metadata preparation,
4. the material may receive timestamp stabilization,
5. the material may be placed into internal containers or slots,
6. the material may proceed toward stable outbound payload exposure.

### 5.3 Admission Does Not Mean

Admission does not mean:

1. the material is already stable,
2. the material is already exposed,
3. the material has entered ASCC,
4. the material has reached an outbound port,
5. the material has passed through Thin C ABI,
6. the material has reached an add-on,
7. the material has been consumed by a receiver.

### 5.4 Admission Boundary Rule

```text
Admission transfers material into ASIP processing authority.
It does not transfer material into communication, delivery, ABI, add-on,
or destination authority.
```

## 7. Admission Rejection

### 7.1 Rejection Definition

Admission rejection occurs when incoming material is not accepted into ASIP lifecycle authority.

Rejection is an ASIP boundary decision.

### 7.2 Rejection Reasons

Admission may be rejected because:

1. the pipeline is unconfigured,
2. the pipeline is stopped,
3. the pipeline is draining,
4. the pipeline is faulted,
5. capacity is unavailable,
6. the material cannot satisfy structural requirements,
7. required metadata is unavailable,
8. required timestamp stabilization is impossible,
9. lifecycle constraints would be violated,
10. configured admission policy rejects the material.

### 7.3 Rejection Non-Meaning

Admission rejection does not mean:

1. a bridge rejected the material,
2. an outbound port rejected the material,
3. Thin C ABI rejected the material,
4. a database rejected the material,
5. telemetry rejected the material,
6. a final receiver rejected the material.

### 7.4 Rejection Rule

```text
Admission rejection is an ASIP-owned decision.
It must not be reported as downstream delivery failure.
```

## 8. Admission Pressure

### 8.1 Admission Pressure Definition

Admission pressure occurs when the pipeline cannot safely accept new material without exceeding ASIP-owned lifecycle constraints.

Admission pressure may be caused by:

1. full containers,
2. exhausted slots,
3. waiting-list saturation,
4. exposed payloads retained too long,
5. pending release records,
6. recycling delay,
7. stabilization backlog,
8. drain mode,
9. fault state.

### 8.2 Pressure Handling Strategies

The pipeline may handle admission pressure by:

1. rejecting new material,
2. delaying admission,
3. applying admission throttling,
4. entering drain mode,
5. prioritizing recycling,
6. reporting diagnostics,
7. applying configured retention expiration,
8. exposing capacity state.

### 8.3 Admission Pressure Non-Meaning

Admission pressure is not:

1. bridge backpressure,
2. outbound port congestion,
3. ABI delay,
4. database backlog,
5. telemetry exporter delay,
6. receiver outage,
7. downstream retry state.

### 8.4 Pressure Rule

```text
Admission pressure describes ASIP’s ability to safely admit material.
It must not be interpreted as final receiver backpressure.
```

## 9. Internal Ownership After Admission

### 9.1 Ownership Definition

After admission, the Independent Pipeline owns the admitted material’s internal lifecycle until the material is rejected, stabilized and exposed, released, expired, or recycled according to ASIP rules.

### 9.2 Owned Lifecycle Responsibilities

The pipeline owns:

1. internal validation participation,
2. metadata preparation,
3. timestamp stabilization,
4. material preparation,
5. container placement,
6. slot occupancy,
7. waiting-list participation,
8. round participation,
9. stabilization,
10. exposure readiness,
11. stable outbound payload exposure,
12. retention until release,
13. recycling after release.

### 9.3 Non-Owned Lifecycle Responsibilities

The pipeline does not own:

1. bridge protocol completion,
2. outbound port delivery completion,
3. ABI transfer completion,
4. add-on projection completion,
5. database write completion,
6. telemetry export completion,
7. receiver acknowledgement,
8. receiver retry lifecycle.

### 9.4 Ownership Rule

```text
ASIP owns internal lifecycle until ASIP release.
It does not own destination lifecycle after exposure.
```

## 10. Stable Payload Retention

### 10.1 Retention Definition

Retention is the ASIP-owned period during which stable outbound payload material remains available after exposure and before ASIP release.

Retention exists to protect the bridge-facing boundary from unstable or prematurely recycled payload material.

### 10.2 Retention Begins

Retention begins when:

1. payload stabilization is complete,
2. payload meaning is mutation-closed,
3. exposure readiness is satisfied,
4. stable outbound payload is exposed,
5. the pipeline records exposure state.

### 10.3 Retention Ends

Retention ends when one of the following ASIP-owned release conditions is satisfied:

1. bridge-facing claim is recorded,
2. configured expiration occurs,
3. exposure is cancelled under ASIP-owned failure policy,
4. fault handling marks the payload unrecoverable,
5. controlled drain/release policy applies.

### 10.4 Retention Rule

```text
Retention preserves stable payload availability for ASIP boundary obligations.
It must not become downstream lifecycle ownership.
```

## 11. Bridge-Facing Claim Boundary

### 11.1 Claim Definition

A bridge-facing claim is the boundary event by which ASCC or a PluginAdapter-facing communication participant claims or admits an exposed stable outbound payload for communication processing.

### 11.2 Claim Meaning

A claim may mean:

1. ASCC has recognized the exposed payload,
2. the bridge-facing participant has obtained a stable view or carrier,
3. ASIP may become eligible for release depending on configured release rules,
4. the payload has crossed from ASIP exposure availability into communication authority.

### 11.3 Claim Non-Meaning

A claim does not mean:

1. bridge protocol has completed,
2. an outbound port has delivered the payload,
3. Thin C ABI has passed the payload,
4. add-ons have interpreted the payload,
5. a receiver has consumed the payload,
6. a database has persisted the payload,
7. telemetry has exported the payload.

### 11.4 Claim Rule

```text
Bridge-facing claim is a boundary event.
It is not final delivery.
```

## 12. Release Model

### 12.1 Release Definition

Release is the ASIP-owned lifecycle transition by which the pipeline determines it no longer needs to retain specific payload material or internal structures for ASIP-owned obligations.

Release is not recycling.

Release authorizes later recycling.

### 12.2 Release Preconditions

Release may require:

1. payload exposure completed,
2. mutation closure preserved,
3. bridge-facing claim recorded or expiration reached,
4. diagnostics recorded where configured,
5. no ASIP-owned observation surface still requires mutable structures,
6. no ASIP-owned retention rule still applies,
7. release policy satisfied.

### 12.3 Release Result

After release, ASIP may:

1. mark exposure record as released,
2. detach internal references,
3. schedule container recycling,
4. schedule slot recycling,
5. clear waiting-list references,
6. reset lifecycle state,
7. preserve immutable diagnostic summaries.

### 12.4 Release Non-Meaning

Release does not mean:

1. external delivery succeeded,
2. ABI passage succeeded,
3. add-on projection succeeded,
4. database persistence succeeded,
5. telemetry export succeeded,
6. receiver consumed the payload.

### 12.5 Release Rule

```text
Release ends ASIP retention obligation.
It does not prove downstream success.
```

## 13. Expiration Model

### 13.1 Expiration Definition

Expiration occurs when an exposed stable outbound payload remains unclaimed or unreleased beyond a configured ASIP retention rule.

Expiration is an ASIP lifecycle decision.

### 13.2 Expiration May Apply To

Expiration may apply to:

1. exposed but unclaimed payloads,
2. retained diagnostic records,
3. waiting exposure records,
4. stale admission attempts,
5. incomplete material under drain or fault policy.

### 13.3 Expiration Result

Expiration may cause:

1. release eligibility,
2. rejection record creation,
3. diagnostic record creation,
4. lifecycle cleanup,
5. recycling eligibility,
6. admission pressure reduction.

### 13.4 Expiration Non-Meaning

Expiration does not mean:

1. downstream rejected the payload,
2. receiver failed,
3. bridge failed,
4. port failed,
5. ABI failed,
6. database failed.

### 13.5 Expiration Rule

```text
Expiration is an ASIP retention policy outcome.
It must not be reported as downstream failure unless a separate downstream
authority reports such failure.
```

## 14. Recycling Model

### 14.1 Recycling Definition

Recycling is the reuse of ASIP-owned internal structures after release conditions are satisfied.

Recycling may apply to:

1. containers,
2. slots,
3. waiting-list entries,
4. round-state records,
5. preparation buffers,
6. exposure records,
7. diagnostic scratch state,
8. internal handles.

### 14.2 Recycling Preconditions

Before recycling, the pipeline must ensure:

1. the material is no longer mutable,
2. exposure obligations are closed,
3. release has occurred,
4. no ASIP-owned view requires the internal structure,
5. no waiting-list entry references the structure,
6. no round manager still tracks the structure,
7. no diagnostic record requires mutable access,
8. recycling will not invalidate stable payload meaning.

### 14.3 Recycling Result

After recycling:

1. containers may become available,
2. slots may become empty,
3. waiting-list records may be cleared,
4. round-state records may be reset,
5. internal counters may be updated,
6. admission capacity may increase,
7. diagnostics may record reuse.

### 14.4 Recycling Non-Meaning

Recycling does not mean:

1. payload was delivered,
2. payload was consumed,
3. payload was persisted,
4. payload was exported,
5. receiver acknowledged payload,
6. downstream lifecycle completed.

### 14.5 Recycling Rule

```text
Recycling reuses ASIP-owned internal structures after release.
It is not proof of downstream delivery.
```

## 15. Container Recycling

### 15.1 Container Recycling Definition

Container recycling is the transition by which an internal pipeline container becomes available for reuse after all ASIP-owned obligations tied to its material are closed.

### 15.2 Container Recycling Preconditions

A container may be recycled only if:

1. all occupied slots are released or cleared,
2. no stable exposure view depends on mutable container state,
3. no waiting list references the container,
4. no round manager is operating on the container,
5. no diagnostic view requires live container internals,
6. release policy permits reuse.

### 15.3 Container Recycling Rule

```text
A container may be reused only after its contents and lifecycle references
are ASIP-released.
```

---

## 16. Slot Recycling

### 16.1 Slot Recycling Definition

Slot recycling is the transition by which an occupied slot becomes available for later placement.

### 16.2 Slot Recycling Preconditions

A slot may be recycled only if:

1. its material is released,
2. its occupancy state is closed,
3. no stable exposure view depends on mutable slot state,
4. no waiting-list entry references the slot,
5. no round manager expects the slot to remain occupied,
6. clearing the slot does not mutate exposed payload meaning.

### 16.3 Slot Recycling Rule

```text
Slot recycling clears internal placement state.
It must not alter stable outbound payload meaning.
```

---

## 17. Waiting-List Recycling

### 17.1 Waiting-List Recycling Definition

Waiting-list recycling clears or reuses waiting-list entries that no longer represent active ASIP-owned lifecycle work.

### 17.2 Waiting-List Recycling Preconditions

A waiting-list entry may be recycled only if:

1. the referenced material was admitted, rejected, released, or expired,
2. the referenced container or slot no longer requires the entry,
3. no round manager depends on the entry,
4. diagnostics have been captured where configured.

### 17.3 Waiting-List Recycling Rule

```text
Waiting-list recycling clears internal coordination records.
It must not erase required lifecycle evidence.
```

---

## 18. Round-State Recycling

### 18.1 Round-State Recycling Definition

Round-state recycling resets or reuses state held by internal round managers after a round has completed, failed, or been cancelled under ASIP-owned lifecycle policy.

### 18.2 Round-State Recycling Preconditions

Round-state may be recycled only if:

1. the round is complete or cancelled,
2. no active transition depends on the round record,
3. all affected material has a valid next state,
4. diagnostics are preserved where required,
5. recycling does not hide unresolved faults.

### 18.3 Round-State Recycling Rule

```text
Round-state recycling may occur only after the round’s lifecycle outcome is
explicitly resolved.
```

## 19. Exposure Record Recycling

### 19.1 Exposure Record Definition

An exposure record tracks ASIP-owned exposure state for a stable outbound payload.

It may contain:

1. payload ID,
2. exposure sequence,
3. stability marker,
4. retention state,
5. claim state,
6. release state,
7. expiration state,
8. diagnostic trace reference.

### 19.2 Exposure Record Recycling Preconditions

An exposure record may be recycled or compacted only if:

1. release has occurred,
2. required diagnostics have been preserved,
3. no ASCC-facing claim process still references it,
4. no ASIP observation surface still requires it,
5. configured retention for trace records has elapsed or been satisfied.

### 19.3 Exposure Record Recycling Rule

```text
Exposure record recycling must preserve required traceability even after
mutable lifecycle state is cleared.
```

## 21. Admission and Recycling Interaction

### 21.1 Interaction Definition

Admission and recycling are linked because available internal capacity depends on whether prior lifecycle structures have been safely released and recycled.

### 21.2 Capacity Relationship

Admission capacity may depend on:

1. available containers,
2. available slots,
3. free waiting-list entries,
4. cleared round-state records,
5. bounded retained exposure records,
6. configured retention budgets,
7. active drain/fault state.

### 21.3 Interaction Rule

```text
Admission must respect recycling reality.
The pipeline must not admit material by assuming unreleased structures are reusable.
```

## 22. Drain Mode Lifecycle

### 22.1 Drain Mode Definition

Drain mode is an ASIP-owned execution mode in which the pipeline stops or restricts new admission while completing already admitted material and releasing internal structures safely.

### 22.2 Drain Mode May Perform

During drain mode, the pipeline may:

1. stop new admission,
2. finish preparation for admitted material,
3. stabilize eligible payloads,
4. expose stable payloads,
5. expire unclaimable payloads under policy,
6. release retained material,
7. recycle internal structures,
8. produce diagnostics.

### 22.3 Drain Mode Must Not Perform

Drain mode must not:

1. wait for final receiver acknowledgement,
2. own outbound port completion,
3. own Thin C ABI completion,
4. perform add-on retry,
5. rewrite destination lifecycle.

### 22.4 Drain Rule

```text
Drain mode completes ASIP-owned lifecycle safely.
It does not drain downstream systems.
```

## 25. Relationship to ASCC

### 25.1 ASCC Boundary

ASCC may claim or admit exposed stable payload material after ASIP exposure.

ASIP may use an ASCC-facing boundary signal only as a release-relevant event if that signal is formally defined as safe for ASIP release.

### 25.2 ASIP Must Not Infer

ASIP must not infer from ASCC claim that:

1. bridge protocol completed,
2. outbound port delivery completed,
3. ABI passage completed,
4. final receiver consumed the payload.

### 25.3 ASCC Rule

```text
ASCC claim may close ASIP retention if configured.
It does not prove final delivery.
```

## 26. Relationship to ASOP

### 26.1 ASOP Boundary

ASOP owns outbound port delivery and Thin C ABI passage.

ASIP release and recycling must not depend on ASOP internals unless a later formal boundary contract provides a safe ASIP-consumable release signal.

### 26.2 ASIP Must Not Own

ASIP must not own:

1. port-local containers,
2. port-local delivery rounds,
3. Thin C ABI transfer state,
4. receiver-facing result handling,
5. port retry backlog.

### 26.3 ASOP Rule

```text
ASIP recycling must not require ownership of outbound port lifecycle.
```

### 27.1 ASAO Boundary

ASAO owns optional add-ons and final receiver semantics beyond the ABI or receiver-facing boundary.

### 27.2 ASIP Must Not Own

5. C# object lifecycle,
6. MQL5 receiver lifecycle,
7. read-only container lifecycle,
8. final receiver acknowledgement.

### 27.3 ASAO Rule

```text
ASIP release and recycling are independent of ASAO destination semantics.
```

## 28. Lifecycle Safety Principles

### 28.1 Admission Safety

The pipeline must not admit material unless it can own its internal lifecycle safely.

### 28.2 Retention Safety

The pipeline must retain exposed stable payload material until ASIP-owned release conditions are satisfied.

### 28.3 Mutation Safety

The pipeline must not mutate stable exposed payload meaning during retention.

### 28.4 Release Safety

The pipeline must release only after ASIP obligations are closed.

### 28.5 Recycling Safety

The pipeline must recycle only after release.

### 28.6 Trace Safety

The pipeline must preserve required diagnostic and trace records even if mutable structures are recycled.

### 28.7 Boundary Safety

The pipeline must not treat downstream lifecycle events as ASIP-owned unless formally admitted through a boundary contract.

### 28.8 Safety Rule

```text
Lifecycle safety means no premature admission, no premature release,
no premature recycling, no mutation after stable exposure, and no downstream
lifecycle ownership.
```

## 33. Summary

The Independent Pipeline lifecycle is governed by:

```text
admission
→ internal ownership
→ preparation
→ stabilization
→ exposure
→ retention
→ bridge-facing claim or expiration
→ release
→ recycling
```

The critical distinctions are:

```text
admission is not delivery
retention is not destination ownership
claim is not final delivery
release is not consumption
recycling is not downstream proof
```

The stable architectural conclusion is:

```text
ASIP owns admission, retention, release, and recycling only within the
Independent Pipeline boundary.

ASIP does not own bridge execution, outbound port delivery, Thin C ABI passage,
add-on projection, receiver acknowledgement, database persistence, telemetry
export, or downstream lifecycle.
```
